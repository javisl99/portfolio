## Context

The starting point was a layered experience. Each team had solved its own area with reasonable local logic, but the overall result forced people to switch screens, reinterpret states, and mentally rebuild the customer story.

The issue was not only visual. It also affected how quickly support and operations could make decisions, and made it harder for product to spot the biggest friction points.

## My contribution

I approached the work as a mix of frontend architecture and product clarification:

- I helped reduce the flow to a smaller set of understandable states,
- proposed a more stable information structure to avoid vertical-specific patches,
- and connected those decisions to a reusable component foundation.

I also documented interface criteria so the next team could extend the workspace without fragmenting it again.

## Key decisions

### A more explicit state model

Before touching the UI, we defined what each critical state actually meant and what decision the interface needed to support in each case.

### Visual hierarchy based on operational priority

The main screen started organizing information by urgency and usefulness instead of by the technical source of the data.

### Components designed for growth

The most repeated blocks were consolidated into system pieces with contracts clear enough to scale without duplication.

> The biggest value was not simply redesigning a screen, but turning a scattered experience into a shared model across teams.

## Outcome

The new workspace made account state easier to read, reduced intermediate steps, and created a stronger base for adding new capabilities without reopening the original consistency problem.
